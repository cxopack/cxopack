# Objections Library

Every sales objection is two things at once: **what they said**, and **what they meant**. Most founders respond to the literal objection, fail, and lose the deal. The objection library's job is to separate the two.

## The method — every objection gets 3 things

1. **The real question underneath.** Objections are rarely the actual blocker; they're a safer way to express a deeper concern.
2. **A peer-tone response.** Not a rebuttal. Not a "I hear you and…" corporate-speak. A response a sharp friend would give.
3. **The qualifying follow-up.** One question that makes them commit or disqualify. This is the half that most sales training misses.

## The 20 objections

### Price-shaped

**"It's too expensive."**
- *Real question:* "Will I get enough value to justify this line item to whoever holds the budget?"
- *Response:* "Compared to what? Right now you're either paying in dollars or paying in <specific cost of not solving it>." Don't discount. Reframe the alternative cost.
- *Follow-up:* "If price weren't the issue, would you move forward?"

**"We don't have budget right now."**
- *Real question:* "Where does this rank vs. our other bets?"
- *Response:* "Understood — most of our customers didn't have budget either. They created it by <specific re-allocation>. What's the category this would come from?"
- *Follow-up:* "When does budget reset? Let's talk <specific date>."

**"Can you do X% discount?"**
- *Real question:* "Can I win this negotiation?" or "Am I buying at a fair price?"
- *Response:* "No on discount. But I can do <something valuable that costs me nothing: extra onboarding, early access, a case-study credit>. Which matters more?"
- *Follow-up:* "If we land on X, are you in?"

### Timing-shaped

**"Send me a proposal / we'll circle back."**
- *Real question:* "How do I get off this call politely?"
- *Response:* "I can write something, but first — on a scale of 1-10, how likely is this to move? If under 7, let's talk about why before I waste your time."
- *Follow-up:* "What has to be true between today and <2 weeks> for this to be a 9?"

**"Not a priority this quarter."**
- *Real question:* "What would make this become one?"
- *Response:* "Fair. What would have to change for it to become a priority next quarter?"
- *Follow-up:* "Mind if I check in on <exact date>?"

**"We just bought something similar."**
- *Real question:* "Do you do something different or is this the same?"
- *Response:* "Got it — how's it going with <tool>? Most people we talk to at this stage either love their tool or wish it did <specific thing>."
- *Follow-up:* "If in 3 months it's not delivering, would you be open to a conversation?"

### Authority-shaped

**"I need to check with my team / boss / co-founder."**
- *Real question:* "I don't want to own this decision alone."
- *Response:* "Of course — what's the best way to make sure that conversation goes well? I can join, send materials, or shape a one-pager they can skim."
- *Follow-up:* "What would they need to see to say yes?"

**"We usually don't work with vendors this small / new."**
- *Real question:* "Is this risky for me professionally?"
- *Response:* "I get it. Here's how <logo> de-risked it: <specific — shorter contract, milestone clauses, pilot>. Would that work for you?"
- *Follow-up:* "If we structure a 90-day trial with a named kill criterion, could you get that approved?"

### Trust-shaped

**"We've been burned by tools like this before."**
- *Real question:* "Why are you different?"
- *Response:* "Tell me about it — what specifically went wrong?" Listen, then address the *actual* pattern they mention, not your standard pitch.
- *Follow-up:* "If we sign, what would you want written into the contract to protect against that happening again?"

**"I haven't heard of you."**
- *Real question:* "Can I trust you with my data / workflow / reputation?"
- *Response:* "Fair. We're <stage> — here's what we have and don't have: <concrete>. If that's not enough, let's find a way to de-risk: <shorter contract / SOC2 statement of effort / customer reference>."
- *Follow-up:* "What would make this work for someone at your company size?"

### Product-shaped

**"It's missing X feature."**
- *Real question:* Either "deal-breaker" or "wishlist." You need to know which.
- *Response:* "Is X a must-have for the first 30 days, or is it a wishlist for later? Those get different answers."
- *Follow-up:* If must-have: "Here's what's in the roadmap and timeline. Want to wait, or can we solve the underlying job differently?" If wishlist: "Great input, noted. On the 80% that works today, does this move forward?"

**"Your integration with Y doesn't cover my use case."**
- *Real question:* Either "specific gap" or "hiding behind this."
- *Response:* Ask for the specific use case. If it's a gap, own it: "You're right. Here's the workaround / timeline for the real fix / what I can commit to." If vague, probe: "Can you walk me through the exact flow that isn't supported?"

**"Your competitor has feature Z."**
- *Real question:* "Am I picking the safe option?"
- *Response:* "They do. We don't. Here's why that's a choice and not an oversight: <your positioning>. If Z is the job you need done, <competitor> is probably your call."
- *Follow-up:* "What's the job that matters most to you in year one?"

### Process-shaped

**"Send me a demo video."**
- *Real question:* "I want to evaluate without committing to a meeting."
- *Response:* "Happy to — but before I do, can I ask what specifically you want to see? Generic demos get forwarded and forgotten; a targeted one lands."
- *Follow-up:* "Can we schedule 20 minutes after you watch to hear your reaction?"

**"Do you have a free trial?"**
- *Real question:* "Will this actually work for me before I pay?"
- *Response:* Depends on your model. If yes: "Yes, here's how to set it up well — most trials fail when <specific mistake>." If no: "We don't. Instead, we do <30-day guarantee / structured pilot / refund>."
- *Follow-up:* "Would you move forward if we can set up a successful first-week with you?"

### Status-quo-shaped

**"We'll just do it in-house / with spreadsheets."**
- *Real question:* "Is this worth outsourcing?"
- *Response:* "Can work for a while. Where it usually breaks is <specific pain that your product solves>. How are you handling that today?"
- *Follow-up:* "What would have to be true for you to outsource this?"

**"Let me think about it."**
- *Real question:* "I don't want to say no to your face."
- *Response:* "Totally fair. Just so I don't bug you unnecessarily — is this a 'think about it and likely yes,' or 'think about it and probably no but I don't want to kill it right now'?"
- *Follow-up:* If the latter: "What specifically is giving you pause?"

**"This is too complex for us."**
- *Real question:* "Can I implement this without a team?"
- *Response:* "I hear this a lot, especially from <their persona>. Here's how <similar customer> got to value in week 1: <specific>. Would you want me to set you up that way?"
- *Follow-up:* "What does 'simple' look like for you?"

### Relationship-shaped

**"I already have someone at <competitor>."**
- *Real question:* "I don't want to damage that relationship."
- *Response:* Respect it: "Good call to stay loyal. When/if that relationship changes, here's my number."
- *Follow-up:* "Do you mind if I check in once in 6 months?"

**"I need to think about the migration."**
- *Real question:* "What does moving off my current tool actually cost?"
- *Response:* Acknowledge the real cost: "Moving is real work. Here's exactly what the <N> customers who switched from <X> had to do: <specific steps, hours, gotchas>. We handle <which steps>."
- *Follow-up:* "If migration were handled, would this move forward?"

## Workflow

When asked about an objection:
1. Identify which of the 20 patterns it matches (or flag as new).
2. Offer the **real question** first — force the founder to see underneath.
3. Offer 1 response they can actually say, in their voice.
4. Offer the **qualifying follow-up**.
5. If the founder says "the response feels awkward" — rewrite in their voice. No corporate-sales-speak.

## Save your own

Every time a new objection comes up in a real deal, add it to `sales/objections/<slug>.md` using the 3-field format. After 20 deals you have a library that's better than any generic one.
