# Positioning (April Dunford method, adapted for solo founders)

## The formula

> For **[audience who struggle with X specific thing]**, **[Product]** is a **[category]** that **[unique value proposition]**. Unlike **[reference point]**, we **[key differentiator with proof]**.

Every blank must be a hard, specific choice. Hedging = bad positioning.

## Workflow

### 1. Alternatives — what are customers doing today?
Not "competitors" — what customers *actually* reach for. Could be a competitor, a spreadsheet, a consultant, or nothing. Force the founder to name their real reference point.

### 2. Unique attributes
List things your product *has* that alternatives don't. Facts, not claims. "Runs on the user's machine" ✅. "Best-in-class UX" ❌.

### 3. Value per attribute
For each attribute, say: "which means…" until you hit a business outcome the customer cares about.

### 4. Who cares
Not everyone cares about every value. Segment: which customer type cares most about each value? Pick the segment where the value hits hardest.

### 5. Category
The category is the mental shelf the product sits on. Wrong shelf = confused customer. Sometimes you create a new category (hard, expensive, usually wrong). Usually you pick an existing one and differentiate within it.

### 6. Assemble the statement
Run it through the formula. Read it out loud. If it sounds like every other landing page in the category, start over.

## Tests

A good positioning statement:
- [ ] Names a **specific struggle**, not a generic problem.
- [ ] Picks a **clear category**.
- [ ] Has a **differentiator with proof** (a fact, not an adjective).
- [ ] Names a **reference point** (the real alternative).
- [ ] Would make a subset of people immediately self-disqualify. If nobody would self-disqualify, the positioning is too broad.

## Output

```md
# Positioning

**For** [who, specific]
**who** [struggle with what]
**[Product]** is a **[category]**
**that** [unique value outcome]
**Unlike** [reference point]
**we** [proof-backed differentiator]

## Proof points (facts, not claims)
1. …
2. …
3. …

## Who is NOT for us (self-disqualifier)
- …
- …

## What we'd stop saying
- [generic claim to drop]
- [feature we'd stop leading with]
```

## Anti-patterns

- "Built for ambitious teams" — vague.
- "The Notion of X" without actually being Notion-shaped.
- Listing 5 differentiators (the customer remembers zero).
- Picking "AI-powered" as a differentiator in a market where everyone's AI-powered.
